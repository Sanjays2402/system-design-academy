Design Payment Processing

Accept money with idempotency, an auditable ledger, and reconciliation across unreliable partners.

SDE245 min50K TPS · zero money loss · multi-day reconciliation
Clarify before drawing

Confirm scope, traffic shape, latency and durability targets, regional requirements, and which operation must remain correct during partial failure.

01 · REQUIREMENTSDefine the contract

Functional

User outcomes

  • Users can initiate and inspect the core payment processing workflow.
  • Operators can configure policy, recover failures, and audit important state changes.
  • Clients can retry safely and observe a stable result.
  • Administrators can disable or repair problematic resources.
Non-functional

System guarantees

  • Meet the stated latency and availability target.
  • Preserve the primary correctness invariant under concurrency.
  • Scale horizontally without one global hot partition.
  • Expose enough telemetry to detect lag, saturation, and loss.

Do not list numbers without consequences. Tie each estimate to a cache, partition, replica, queue, or consistency decision.

03 · CONTRACTAPI and data model

POST /v1/payment-system/operations
Idempotency-Key: 5d6f...
{ "actor_id": "u_123", "payload": {...} }

GET /v1/payment-system/{transaction_id}
GET /v1/payment-system/{transaction_id}/status

Primary record

transaction_id, owner/tenant, state, version, timestamps, policy, and an idempotency key. Keep a version for conditional writes.

Event record

event_id, aggregate ID, event type, occurred_at, actor, schema version, and trace ID. Events are immutable and replayable.

05 · CRITICAL FLOWRequest lifecycle

Accept safely

Authenticate, validate, apply quotas, and resolve idempotency before expensive work.

Coordinate

Route by a stable partition key and claim mutable state with a lease or conditional write.

Commit the invariant

Persist the smallest authoritative state before acknowledging success.

Fan out side effects

Publish analytics, notifications, indexing, and audit work asynchronously.

Observe and repair

Track latency, errors, lag, saturation, and reconciliation gaps.

07 · DECISIONSTrade-offs

DecisionPrefer whenCost
Strong vs eventual consistencyUse strong consistency for ownership, inventory, money, aliases, and state transitions.Coordination latency and reduced availability during partitions.
Sync vs async processingKeep only user-visible correctness on the synchronous path.Async side effects need idempotency, lag monitoring, and replay.
SQL vs distributed KV/logChoose by access pattern and invariant, not fashion.Transactions simplify correctness; distributed stores simplify scale.

08 · PRODUCTIONFailures, security, and observability

Failure posture

  • Timeout every dependency.
  • Retry only safe operations with jitter.
  • Use circuit breakers and admission control.
  • Reconcile durable truth after recovery.

Security posture

  • Authenticate at ingress; authorize per resource.
  • Encrypt in transit and at rest.
  • Redact sensitive logs.
  • Audit privileged mutations.

Golden signals

  • Rate, errors, and latency percentiles.
  • Queue lag and oldest-item age.
  • Cache hit ratio and shard skew.
  • Replica lag and reconciliation gaps.
